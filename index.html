Crafting an Effective CV

A CV is more than a document; it’s how I introduce myself professionally. This lecture focused on formatting, tailoring, and storytelling that earn interviews.

Introduction

We explored what makes a CV professional: clear structure, relevant content, and a tone that reflects my strengths. It reminded me that my CV is often the first (and sometimes only) impression an employer gets.

Purpose & Importance

A CV summarises personal info, education, experience, projects, skills, and wins. When it’s well-organised, it makes it easy for hiring teams to understand my fit within seconds.

Making a Strong First Impression

  • Use a clear, professional layout.
  • Highlight the most relevant achievements.
  • Keep fonts, spacing, and styling consistent.
  • Stay concise—ideally one or two pages.

Tailoring Checklist

Step Action
Read the job description Highlight the exact skills, tools, and experiences they want.
Prioritise relevance Remove anything that doesn’t serve this application.
Use simple design Stick to easy fonts (Arial, Calibri), 10–12pt text, and consistent spacing.
Keep it short One to two pages with punchy bullet points and measurable impact.

Personal Information

Full name, phone, professional email, optional LinkedIn. Skip personal details like ID numbers or home addresses.

Quick Summary

A short paragraph about who I am, key strengths, what I want to do, and one proud win.

Employment History

Reverse chronological roles with titles, places, timelines, and standout accomplishments.

Education

Highest degree first, school, dates, and relevant modules or capstone projects (like my BSc in Software Engineering at SLIIT City Campus, graduating 2027).

Skills & Extras

Technical skills (HTML, CSS, Python, Java, SQL), soft skills (communication, teamwork, time management), languages, certifications, awards, and projects.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Spelling and grammar errors, outdated info, messy layouts, unprofessional email addresses, or CVs that feel too generic.

Lecture 03

Cultivating Effective Research Skills

Research is just finding stuff out in an organized way to answer questions or fix problems. It lets us really get what's going on and come up with new thoughts. Being good at research is key for school, projects, and making smart calls.

Introduction

Research is just finding stuff out in an organized way to answer questions or fix problems. It lets us really get what's going on and come up with new thoughts. Being good at research is key for school, projects, and making smart calls.

What is a Research?

Research is gathering info carefully and in order. It's about looking at facts, spotting trends, and using what you know to make new ideas or make old ones better.

Key Takeaways

  • Research requires planning, critical thinking, and organization
  • It's more than just Googling—it's a step-by-step process
  • Proper structure and citation are essential
  • Clear objectives guide effective research

Parts of a Research Paper

Component Description
Title What the research is mainly about
Author(s) Who did the research
Table of Contents A list of what's inside
Declaration & Acknowledgement Saying it's your own work and thanking people
Abstract A quick summary of the whole study
Introduction Why the research matters and some background
Literature Review What other studies found
Methodology How the research was done
Findings/Results What you found out
Conclusion & Recommendations Final thoughts and what you might do next
References Where you got your info

Starting a Research Study

The intro gets people into the topic by adding:

  • Background: The basic stuff you need to know
  • Context: The big picture
  • Relevance: Why it matters
  • Problem Statement: What the research is trying to solve

Literature Review

A literature review:

  • Sums up what's already out there
  • Shows what others have learned
  • Points out holes or things we need to know more about
  • Helps guide where your research goes

Research Goals

Research goals are clear and to-the-point. They help keep the research on track. They say exactly what you're trying to do.

Research Questions

These are the questions your research tries to answer.

Hypotheses

Guesses that you can test while you're doing the research.

How You Do It (Methodology)

The how-you-do-it part tells:

  • Research Design: What kind of study it is (like, asking people questions)
  • Participants: Who was in the study and why you picked them
  • Data Collection: How you got the info (like, doing interviews)
  • Data Analysis: How you looked at the data
  • Ethical Things: Making sure people are safe, keeping their info private, and getting their okay to be in the study

Wrapping Up

The end bit sums up what you found, notes what could be better, and gives final thoughts or ideas for more studies.

What means Abstract?

An abstract gives you the quick rundown of the whole study. It helps people see if the research is up their alley.

How an Abstract Is Set Up

  • Background
  • Purpose
  • Methods
  • Main things you found
  • Conclusion

Personal Reflection

This talk helped me see that research is more than just Googling—it's a step-by-step thing that takes planning, thinking hard, and getting organized. I saw how important it is to kick things off with a real problem, look at old studies, set goals, and pick the right way to get info. I also got why parts like the abstract, literature review, and how-you-did-it are important in a research paper.

All in all, this lesson made me feel better about reading, getting, and doing research. It showed me how to work in order, think harder, and share info clearly and like a pro.

Online & Office Etiquette

Etiquette is just knowing how to act in social situations, including at work. Online etiquette, or netiquette, means being polite, professional, and responsible when you're talking to people through emails, chats, and video calls.

What's Good Etiquette?

Etiquette is just knowing how to act in social situations, including at work. Online etiquette, or netiquette, means being polite, professional, and responsible when you're talking to people through emails, chats, and video calls.

Why it's Important:

  • It helps you communicate easier
  • It keeps things professional and respectful at work
  • It avoids confusion
  • It makes you and your company look good
  • It builds trust and teamwork

What is Email Etiquette?

Email etiquette refers to the proper and professional way of writing emails. Since emails represent us in a formal setting, the words we choose, the tone we use, and the structure of our message show our respect, professionalism, and seriousness. A well-written email creates a good impression and helps us communicate clearly.

Why Use Emails?

  • Quick, professional communication
  • Sharing info for work or personal reasons
  • Keeping track of conversations
  • Staying positive and helpful

Basic Online Rules

Emails are normal for work communication, so:

Rule Guideline
Rule 01 Make your subject line clear and start with a name
Rule 02 Don't use All capitals, cause it looks like yelling
Rule 03 Be careful about sending personal stuff
Rule 04 Watch out for being too casual; keep it professional
Rule 05 Reread your email before sending to be sure it makes sense
Rule 06 Use good grammar so you sound serious
Rule 07 Add a note when you forward something so people know why
Rule 08 Ignore those forward this emails; they're not work appropriate

Phone Etiquette

Phone etiquette shows how to act on the phone at work or in social settings.

Main rules:

  • Let everyone talk; don't interrupt
  • Silence your phone in meetings
  • Step away to take personal calls in a shared space
  • Don't use your phone on conference calls if you don't need to
  • Set up voicemail so you don't miss calls
  • Go somewhere private for personal talks
  • Don't browse social media or play games during meetings
  • Keep phones out of bathrooms and meeting rooms

Why do we need to follow these rules?

  • Makes for respectful, professional conversation
  • Less distractions, more focus
  • Shows you're professional and thoughtful

Video Call Etiquette

  • Dress like you would for work, even at home
  • Check your setup beforehand
  • Mute when you're not talking to reduce noise
  • Pay attention during the meeting

Instant Messaging

  • Keep messages short
  • Reply quickly
  • Don't use too many emojis or slang at work

Social Media for Work

  • Don't post secret work information
  • Be professional in your posts
  • Ask before sharing other people's content

Professional tone of voice

Clear, polite, and respectful

Active listening

Focusing fully on the caller's concerns

Empathy

Understanding the caller's feelings and responding kindly

Patience

Staying calm with confused or upset callers

Accurate information handling

Taking messages and giving correct details

Proper greetings and closings

Opening and ending calls in a professional way

Handling pressure

Keeping composure during urgent or challenging calls

Personal Reflection

From this lecture, I have learned that practicing proper online and telephone etiquette is crucial for building a professional and respectful work environment. Following these rules ensures clear communication, prevents misunderstandings, and portrays a disciplined and responsible image.

I also realized that etiquette extends beyond emails and phone calls—it applies to video calls, instant messaging, and social media, which are increasingly used in professional settings. Learning these practices helps me develop awareness, patience, and adaptability in various communication scenarios. By consistently applying proper etiquette, I can enhance my personal and professional credibility, strengthen workplace relationships, and contribute positively to team dynamics.

Meetings

A meeting is the coming together of people to discuss issues, arrive at decisions, share information, or solve problems. Meetings are a very vital component of any organization for effective communication and decision-making.

What is a Meeting?

A meeting is the coming together of people to discuss issues, arrive at decisions, share information, or solve problems. Meetings are a very vital component of any organization for effective communication and decision-making.

Purpose of Meetings

  • To understand situations and exchange ideas
  • To inform and explain new strategies or updates
  • To give and receive feedback on ideas
  • To plan, train, and prepare for actions
  • To resolve conflicts and misunderstandings

Types of Meetings

Category Type Description
Based on Formality Formal Meeting It follows a fixed agenda and pre-announcement is needed
Informal Meeting This can be held without a prior written notice. This is more casual and flexible
Based on Purpose Informational Meeting A gathering where information is provided to participants
Consultative Meeting To seek opinions and solutions from members
Executive Meeting Decisions are made by those in authority
Company Meetings First General Meeting Within 18 months of registration (legally required)
Annual General Meeting (AGM) This is a yearly meeting that reviews the progress of the company
Extraordinary General Meeting (EGM) It is held whenever there is a pressing matter to be considered
Board Meetings Meetings of directors, usually monthly; at least four times a year
Committee Meetings Small groups formed by the board to handle specific tasks
Creditors Meetings Arranged to protect creditors' rights, sometimes by court order

Key Meeting Vocabulary

  • Agenda: A list of topics to be discussed
  • Minutes: Written record of what was discussed and decided
  • Action Items: Tasks assigned to specific people
  • Participants: People attending the meeting
  • Decisions: Conclusions reached during the meeting

Meeting Preparation Checklist

  • Define the purpose and agenda
  • Identify participants
  • Arrange date, time, and venue or virtual link
  • Prepare any necessary documents and materials

Preparing Minutes

These are notes summarizing the key points, decisions, and action items of a meeting.

Purpose:

  • Record attendees and discussions
  • Track decisions, next steps, and responsibilities
  • Serve as reference for absent participants

What to Include in Minutes:

  • Date and time of the meeting
  • List of Participants
  • Agenda items and discussion topics
  • Decisions made
  • Action items and responsible persons

Personal Reflection

This lecture taught me that meetings are not mere formalities; they are, in fact, the most vital tool for communication, decision-making, and problem-solving in organizations. Preparing agendas, documenting minutes, and following through with action items all are important from this point onward to ensure that meetings yield desired results. The understanding of different types of meetings helped in the way companies structure the communication and make decisions by engaging various stakeholders. I now feel quite confident in participating in or organizing meetings professionally.

Dining Etiquette

Dining etiquette is a set of rules and manners that guide polite and respectful behavior at the dining table. This reflects professionalism, upbringing, and respect for other people, especially in settings that are formal, such as business meetings or any special occasions.

What is Dining Etiquette?

Dining etiquette is a set of rules and manners that guide polite and respectful behavior at the dining table. This reflects professionalism, upbringing, and respect for other people, especially in settings that are formal, such as business meetings or any special occasions. Good dining etiquette is not about being fancy; rather, it should make others feel comfortable and respected.

Key Skills Practiced

  • Using cutlery, glasses, and spoons properly
  • Dressing appropriately for meals
  • Waiting for everyone before starting
  • Maintaining polite conversation and posture
  • Showing respect and consideration to others

Simple Rules and Etiquettes

  • Make eye contact during conversation
  • Do not put bags, sunglasses, cell phones, or briefcases on the table
  • Keep your napkin on your lap throughout the meal
  • Chew with your mouth closed and do not speak with food in your mouth
  • Pass salt and pepper together
  • Handle money politely and patiently

Common Table Settings

  • Work from the outside in, using the outermost utensils for the first course and working your way inward with each subsequent course
  • Typical placement: Water, dinner fork, napkin, dinner knife, teaspoon, cup & saucer, dessert utensils
  • Bread and drinks: Just remember the simple trick: left hand makes a "b" for bread, right hand makes a "d" for drink

Additional Rules & Etiquette

  • Burp or sneeze quietly into your napkin over your mouth; never blow your nose at the table
  • Excuse yourself only after the host indicates that the meal is finished
  • Advise host in advance of any allergies, not upon arrival
  • Handle food or service problems in a discreet and polite manner
  • Never point out others' mistakes, but lead by example

Personal Reflection

This lecture taught me that dining etiquette goes beyond following rules; it is a matter of respect, mindfulness, and making others comfortable. I have learned table manners, which have helped me feel confident in formal and professional settings while showing courtesy and consideration to those around me. Proper etiquette not only leaves a positive impression but fosters social grace and professionalism as well.

What is Emotional Intelligence?

Emotional Intelligence (EQ) is basically being able to:

  • Know what you're feeling and why
  • Get what other people are feeling
  • Deal with your feelings in a good way

Why Should We Care?

Emotional Intelligence makes it easier to talk to people, make good choices, have good relationships, and handle tough times with grit and care.

Getting Emotions

Emotions are just what you feel when something happens, or you think about something. They help you decide what to do, get close to people, and know yourself. Feelings are part of being human, and it's okay to feel them!

What Makes Up EQ (According to Goleman)

  • Knowing Yourself – Seeing what you feel
  • Handling Yourself – Dealing with your feelings

  • People Skills – Having good relationships

Project Proposal

A project proposal is basically a document that spells out what a project is all about, how it's gonna work, and what we hope to get out of it. It's a way to get everyone on the same page so they can decide if it's worth doing.

Introduction

A project proposal is basically a document that spells out what a project is all about, how it's gonna work, and what we hope to get out of it. It's a way to get everyone on the same page so they can decide if it's worth doing.

What's a Project Proposal?

Think of a proposal as a document that answers these questions:

  • What's the project?
  • Why do we need it?
  • How are we gonna do it?
  • What are the goals, steps, schedule, and what we expect to see at the end?

It's all about making sure everyone knows what's up before they say yes.

Why Write a Project Proposal?

  • Makes the project idea clear
  • Gives you a plan to stick to
  • Helps you get the okay, money, or help you need
  • Spots any problems early on
  • Means everyone knows what they're doing and what the project's about

What Goes Into a Project Proposal

Component Description
Background & Problem Statement Describe the problem or need that the project will address and explain why it is an important issue or need. This section sets the context and justifies the project.
Objectives State what the project wants to achieve. Objectives should be SMART: specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound.
Scope & Methodology Describe the tasks, activities, and methods that will be used to conduct the project, which delineates the boundaries and approach of the work.
Deliverables List the expected outcomes or final results of the project. Be specific about what will be produced or accomplished.
Timeline & Budget Provide a schedule for completing tasks and an estimated cost for resources. Include milestones and key deadlines.
Conclusion Summarize the benefits and importance of the project. Reinforce why the project should be approved and supported.

Importance of Writing a Project Proposal

Writing a project proposal is important because it:

  • Helps communicate your project idea clearly
  • Provides a structured plan to follow
  • Helps secure approval, funding, or support
  • Identifies risks, resources, and requirements early
  • Ensures everyone involved understands their goals and responsibilities

What I Got Out of This

I now know that a good project proposal has to be easy to understand, well-organized, and focused on what you want to achieve. It should explain the problem, lay out the goals, list what you'll produce, and have a reasonable schedule and budget.

Knowing how to write good proposals makes you a better communicator, helps you plan projects better, and lets you sell your ideas more easily.

Personal Reflection

This lecture helped me understand the structure and importance of project proposals. I learned that a well-written proposal is essential for getting projects approved and ensuring everyone understands the goals and expectations. The SMART objectives framework and clear component structure will be valuable tools for future project planning.

Activity Description

Placed 200 kilometers from our main ship after a crash-landing on the surface of the moon, we would be affected by limited oxygen, extreme temperatures of -173°C to +127°C, and no Earth communication. There were eight in our team, and we all had to work together to choose 6 essential items out of the 14 available to ensure our survival on the way back to the mother ship.

Team Members

  • H.P.K.D Pinnapola
  • D.H Ishwarage
  • S.N.N Shashikala
  • K.K.I.A Kavindi
  • H.G.A.I Bosilu
  • S.M.O.N Semasighe
  • P.P.S.N.T. Perera
  • S.G.N. Oneka

Items Selected & Rationale

By discussion, we have strategically chosen:

Item Assigned To Rationale
1. Oxygen Tank (3 hour supply) H.P.K.D Pinnapola Most basic survival requirement in an airless atmosphere
2. Solar-Powered Heating Blanket D.H Ishwarage Against extreme temperatures of the lunar night
3. Water Recycling Pack S.N.N Shashikala Essential for maintaining hydration during 200 km trek
4. Lightweight Lunar Tent K.K.I.A Kavindi Multipurpose shelter from radiation and temperature extremes
5. Lunar GPS Device H.G.A.I Bosilu Essential navigation tool across featureless lunar terrain
6. Robotic Assistant S.M.O.N Semasighe Multifunctional tool useful for repairs and heavy lifting

Items We Excluded

We will also deliberately exclude freeze-dried food (journey too short), toolkit (robotic assistant provides cover), mission flag (purely symbolic), moon goggles (non-critical), energy gel packs (less crucial than water), signal booster (unreliable at distance), journal and pencil (non-essential), and emergency flare (P.P.S.N.T. Perera/S.G.N. Oneka) because it is of no use at 200 km distance.

Key Learnings

  • Communication & Active Listening: We learned to articulate reasoning persuasively while remaining open to diverse perspectives and fostering mutual respect and understanding within the team.
  • Decision Making through Collaboration: The exercise showed that effective decisions arise out of collective intelligence. We learned to build consensus by way of compromise and finding common ground despite starting with opposing opinions.
